What is BMR?

Definition & Purpose of BMR

BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) is the amount of calories your body needs to perform vital functions, such as breathing, circulation, cell production, and temperature management at rest. So essentially, BMR is the minimum amount of calories your body needs to function properly and survive.

What Influences BMR?

BMR is not a fixed value. Based on several factors, BMR can change greatly. Some of these factors include height, weight, age, and gender. For example, individuals with more lean body muscle need to consume more2 because their muscle tissue consume more energy, even at rest.

Here is a list of factors that influence BMR.

  • Age
  • Weight
  • Gender
  • Height
  • Pregnancy
  • Hormones
  • Genetics
  • Temperature Exposure
  • Illness
  • Medications

How is BMR Calculated?

In a clinical setting, BMR is calculated after your body goes into a complete state of rest, is in a thermoneutral environment (not too hot nor too cold), and after a period of fasting. These conditions ensure that digestion and physical activity do not interfere with diagnosis.

However, in a general setting, like your home. You will not have access to such clinical-grade technology. In this case, formulas are your best option. There are several formulas to choose from, like the Mifflin-St Jeor Formula, or the Harris-Benedict Formula1, which both use age, height, and weight to estimate. But the most accurate formula would be the Mifflin-St Jeor Formula.

Why Does BMR Matter?

BMR is essential for managing your health, nutrition, and weight effectively.

By understanding your BMR, you can:

  • Plan meals effectively: Ensure you're eating enough to support vital functions without overeating.
  • Set realistic weight goals: Adjust your calorie intake to lose, maintain, or gain weight.
  • Improve fitness outcomes: Align your training and nutrition with your metabolic needs.
  • Avoid under-eating: Prevent fatigue, hormonal imbalances, and slowed metabolism caused by extreme calorie restriction.

In short, BMR is your metabolic baseline. Knowing it allows you to control your energy intake more precisely and make smarter decisions about your health and fitness.

Using your BMR

BMR is just the base for many other properties. Once you know your BMR, you can use it as a detail to estimate different kinds of caloric needs & energy metabolism values.

Calculate TDEE with BMR

Using your BMR, you can estimate your TDEE aswell. The calculation table has been provided.

  • Sedentary – Little to no exercise, desk job. Multiplier: 65
  • Lightly Active – Light exercise 1–3 days/week. Multiplier: 118
  • Moderately Active – Moderate exercise 3–5 days/week. Multiplier: 3120
  • Very Active – Hard exercise 6–7 days/week. Multiplier: 6940
  • Extra Active – Very intense training or physical job. Multiplier: 1910
